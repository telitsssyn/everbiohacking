Physical activity

Integrating physical activity into your life allows you to achieve four key systemic improvements:

  • Longevity & Mortality: Clinical data demonstrates a 30-40% reduction in all-cause premature mortality risk. Movement is your primary insurance policy against biological decay.
  • Cognitive functions: Improved brain function and memory in all age groups
  • Immunological Resilience: Strengthening the immune system and anti-inflammatory state.
  • Mental Fortitude: Moving your body is a frontline defense against neurochemical imbalances, reducing the risk of depression by 20–30%.

A Balanced Training Protocol

To feel your best today and ensure your body stays strong for decades to come, you don’t need to spend hours in the gym every day. Success lies in balancing three specific types of movement, each serving a unique role in your long-term health.

1. Steady-State Endurance (Zone 2)

This is the foundation of your health. It involves low-intensity activity where you move at a pace that allows you to carry on a full conversation without getting out of breath.

  • The Benefit: It teaches your body to use energy more efficiently, strengthens your heart, and helps your cells recover faster. Think of it as your daily "recharge" for long-lasting energy.
  • The Routine: 3–4 sessions per week, lasting 45–90 minutes each.
  • Examples: Brisk walking, light jogging, cycling, or swimming at a comfortable pace.
  • The Simple Test: If you can’t speak in full sentences while moving, you’re going too fast.

2. Peak Heart Health (VO2 Max)

This is about testing your limits once a week. Your VO2 Max (the maximum amount of oxygen your body can use) is one of the most powerful predictors of how many healthy years you have ahead of you.

  • The Benefit: It keeps your heart strong and your blood vessels elastic. It is essentially "anti-aging" for your cardiovascular system.
  • The Routine: 1 session per week, lasting about 30–40 minutes total.
  • The 4x4 Method:
    1. 4 minutes of high-intensity effort (where you are breathing heavily).
    2. 4 minutes of easy recovery (walking or light movement).
    3. Repeat 4 times.
  • Why it works: These short bursts of intensity "wake up" your system and significantly improve your overall fitness level.

3. Strength and Resilience (Resistance Training)

Muscle is more than just a physical look—it is your body's most important metabolic organ. As we age, we naturally lose muscle mass, so strength training acts as "armor" for your skeleton and metabolism.

  • The Benefit: It builds strong bones, improves posture, and helps manage blood sugar levels. A stronger body is more resistant to injury and recovers from stress much faster.
  • The Routine: 2–3 sessions per week.
  • The Focus: Stick to the basics—squats, lunges, pushing, and pulling movements. You don't need to lift extreme weights; the goal is consistent, meaningful resistance.
  • Why it matters: Maintaining muscle is one of the best ways to stay independent and active throughout your entire life.

A few important tips:

1. Injury Prevention

Injuries can create serious limitations, create body imbalances and cause long term complications. It’s worth being cautious even if it means not doing certain things. 

2. Habit Formation

Create an exercise habit. Do it everyday, no matter what (unless you’re injured, have a limiting medical condition, or under doctor’s orders). You don’t even think about it. It just happens out of habit. Do not give yourself the option to decide. 

3. Balanced Training

Work on strength training, cardio, balance and flexibility.

4. Daily Activity Integration

Move throughout the day – after each meal, be active for 5-10 minutes. Every 30 minutes, get up from your desk and move around a bit.

5. Practical Execution over Analysis

Don’t get caught up and paralyzed in all the nuances and rabbit holes of exercise. Being active, in whatever ways you can, pays big for your health.
