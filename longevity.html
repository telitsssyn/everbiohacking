Longevity and Rejuvenation

In previous sections, we have thoroughly analyzed the fundamental pillars of health: sleep hygiene, systematic training, nutrient-dense nutrition, and mental well-being. It is crucial to understand that these foundational factors account for 80–90% of success in slowing down the aging process. Without a solid foundation, any pharmacological intervention will yield extremely low efficiency.

However, modern longevity science (Geroscience) is actively investigating molecules capable of influencing the biological mechanisms of aging. These substances—ranging from nutraceuticals to prescription medications—represent the "front line" of biohacking. At present, many of them are in clinical trials or have an evidence base limited to model organisms. Therefore, their use requires extreme caution and a deep understanding of the associated risks.

1. Metabolic Regulators and mTOR Path Modulators

These drugs were originally developed to treat specific diseases but have shown potential in extending lifespan by influencing cellular metabolism.

  • Rapamycin: Perhaps the most promising candidate. It inhibits the mTOR protein, which governs cell growth and division. By slowing this process, rapamycin stimulates autophagy (cellular self-cleaning). Risks: immunosuppression, impact on glucose levels
  • Metformin: A classic drug for treating type 2 diabetes. It is hypothesized to mimic the effects of caloric restriction and reduce systemic inflammation.
  • SGLT2 Inhibitors (Gliflozins): Medications for kidney and heart health that help the body excrete excess glucose. In animal studies, they have shown a significant impact on survival rates.

2. NAD+ Precursors and Energy Metabolism

As we age, levels of the NAD+ molecule in cells steadily decline, leading to mitochondrial degradation and impaired DNA repair.

  • Nicotinamide Riboside (NR) and NMN: Two forms of NAD+ precursors. Research is aimed at determining whether they can effectively raise these levels in humans and improve metabolic health.
  • Ca-AKG (Calcium Alpha-Ketoglutarate): Involved in the Krebs cycle and epigenetic regulation. Data suggests it may be able to slow down the "epigenetic clock" of aging.

3. Senolytics: Disposal of "Zombie Cells"

Senescent cells are aging cells that have stopped dividing but have not died. Instead, they secrete toxic substances that "poison" neighboring healthy cells.

  • Fisetin and Quercetin: Plant flavonoids with potential senolytic activity.
  • Dasatinib: A potent anti-cancer drug that, in combination with quercetin, is being tested for the radical removal of senescent cells. Warning: High toxicity; requires strict medical supervision.

4. Cellular Regeneration and Protection

Substances in this group work at the organelle level, supporting the structural integrity of the organism.

  • Urolithin A: A derivative of gut microbiota (from pomegranate ellagitannins). It stimulates mitophagy—the renewal of the cell's "power plants."
  • Spermidine: A natural compound that triggers autophagy. Found in certain foods, it is used in biohacking to enhance cellular cleansing.
  • Taurine and L-Carnosine: Taurine is critical for heart and muscle health, while L-Carnosine protects proteins from glycation (damage caused by sugars).
  • GlyNAC (Glycine + N-Acetylcysteine): A combination that raises levels of glutathione—the body's master antioxidant, protecting cells from oxidative stress.
  • Lithium (Microdosing): Research indicates that extremely low doses of lithium may provide neuroprotective effects and correlate with increased lifespan in certain populations.

Conclusion: A Risk-Oriented Approach

The main challenge of longevity biohacking is the lack of 50-year longitudinal studies on humans taking these substances. Most data is derived from mice or short-term clinical tests.

Core Principles of Caution:

  • Do No Harm: Pharmacological interventions have side effects that may outweigh potential benefits.
  • Individual Context: What helps one organism may be counterproductive for another due to genetic differences or current health status.
  • Biomarker Monitoring: Any intervention must be accompanied by regular blood tests and organ function monitoring.

Biohacking is not about finding a "magic pill," but rather the precise tuning of a complex biological system, where pharmacology serves only as a supplement to a conscious lifestyle.
